MYP4 History Notes

1. What is History? ▼

History = the study, reconstruction and interpretation of the past through primary sources
  • Historians must be as objective as possible (no personal bias)
  • They analyse, evaluate and interpret primary sources for reliability
  • They also use secondary sources to understand other interpretations

Can history change? Yes — new sources emerge, existing sources are reinterpreted, leading to new conclusions.

Key perspectives: historic events, culture, politics, economy, technology, outstanding figures.

2. Primary & Secondary Sources ▼

Primary Sources
Created AT THE TIME of the event. Letters, diaries, newspapers, speeches, photographs, artifacts, firsthand accounts.
Secondary Sources
Created LATER, based on primary sources. History books, academic articles, documentaries, encyclopedias, biographies.

Key difference: Primary = firsthand, no interpretation. Secondary = analysed/interpreted by others.

A newspaper from 1850 = primary source. A history book about 1850 = secondary source.

3. OPVL — Source Analysis Framework ▼

O — Origin
WHO created the source? WHEN was it created? (e.g. Delacroix, 1830)
P — Purpose
WHY was it created? To inform? To persuade? To celebrate? To record?
V — Value
What makes it USEFUL for a historian? First-hand experience? Created close to event? Shows a perspective?
L — Limitations
What makes it LESS reliable? Bias? Subjectivity? Limited perspective? Created too close (emotions)?

Key source: "Liberty Leading the People" by Eugène Delacroix (1830) — a painting celebrating the French revolutionary spirit. Valuable as a contemporary artistic source; limited because it is politically motivated, not an objective record.

4. Types of Revolutions ▼

Revolution = widespread, radical, far-reaching change that impacts society in many ways
1. Political
Change of political system (e.g. French, Russian, American Revolutions)
2. Social
Radical change in social structure, class relations, equality
3. Economic / Technological
Application of new knowledge transforming economy (e.g. Industrial Revolution)
4. Cultural / Intellectual
New ideas in art, literature, thinking (e.g. Renaissance, Enlightenment, Cubism)
Most revolutions involve confrontation, radical change, and a desire to make people's lives better.

5. Political Revolutions ▼

French Revolution (1789)
Ended absolutism & Louis XVI. New democratic republic. Ended nobility privileges. Birth of French nationalism.
Russian Revolution (1917)
Led by Lenin & Trotsky. Ended Tsarism (Nicholas II). Soviet Union founded (1922). First communist state.
American Revolution (1775-1783)
Independence from Britain. USA founded 1783. New democratic nation born.
Key names to memorise: Louis XVI, Lenin, Nicholas II, Trotsky. All three revolutions used force/violence.

6. Cultural & Intellectual Revolutions ▼

Renaissance (14th-16th c.)
Italy. Artists: Leonardo da Vinci, Michelangelo. Realism, perspective, human emotions in art. Dante wrote in Italian (not Latin) = HUMANISM = literature for ordinary people.
Cubism (early 20th c.)
Pablo Picasso. Objects broken into geometric shapes. Revolutionary break from artistic tradition.
Enlightenment (17th-18th c.)
Locke, Montesquieu, Rousseau. Natural rights (liberty, property, equality). Reason over religion. Challenged absolute monarchy. Power from the people, not God.

7. Industrial Revolution: Causes ▼

First Industrial Revolution: Britain, 1750-1850

Natural Resources
Britain had abundant coal and iron — essential for steam engines and steel production
Agricultural Revolution
Increased food production; freed rural workers who moved to cities to work in factories
Geography
Rivers and ports facilitated trade; island nation with strong navy and merchant fleet
Colonial Empire
Provided raw materials AND markets for manufactured goods

8. Industrial Revolution: Consequences ▼

Positive
Railways = faster & cheaper transport. Mass production. Economic growth. New cities. More goods available.
Negative (social)
Poor working conditions. Child labour. Long hours. Poverty for workers. Urban overcrowding. Pollution.

New social classes: Industrial working class (proletariat) + Industrial middle class (bourgeoisie).

Urbanisation: mass movement from rural areas to industrial cities.

Essay Option 1 = Causes | Essay Option 2 = Consequences. Know BOTH in depth.

9. Mesopotamia & Sumer Innovations ▼

1. Cuneiform Writing
First writing system. Used for trade records, contracts, scientific knowledge.
2. Farming Innovations
Irrigation systems, dams, dikes. The plow. Surplus crops → cities grew.
3. The Wheel
First used for pottery, then transport. Improved trade over land.
4. Sail & Sailboats
Wind-powered travel. Faster trade along rivers/seas. Boosted economy and cultural exchange.

5. Metallurgy & Brickmaking: Copper and bronze tools, weapons, jewellery. Mud bricks for stronger buildings, temples, city walls.

10. Research Questions (MYP Format) ▼

Perfect MYP RQ: CLEAR · FOCUSED/NARROW · RESEARCHABLE · OPEN-ENDED · FEASIBLE

Example: "How did industrialization impact negatively the daily lives of workers during the First Industrial Revolution (1750-1850) in Britain?"

  • Geographic focus: Britain (specific)
  • Time focus: 1750-1850 (specific)
  • Open-ended: starts with "How" (encourages analysis)
  • Narrow: focuses on workers + negative impacts only
  • Connected to 1 MYP Global Context + 1 Key Concept
Too broad: "How did industrialisation affect people's life?" — no place, no time, too general.

11. Migration ▼

Push Factors (leaving)
Poverty, famine, political repression, lack of jobs, violence, discrimination
Pull Factors (attracting)
High living standards, job opportunities, democracy, better education, safety

Positive impacts of migrants on host country: fill labour shortages, pay taxes, bring skills, start businesses, boost demand, send remittances.

Diaspora: a group of people from the same country living outside their homeland while keeping connections to it.

Remittance: money sent by migrants back to their home country.

12. Essay Writing Structure (PEEL) ▼

1Introduction

General opening sentence → Background → Thesis statement (main argument) → Essay plan (what each paragraph will cover)

2Body Paragraphs (PEEL)

Point (topic sentence) → Evidence (facts, examples, quotes) → Explanation (how evidence supports the point) → Link (to thesis/next paragraph)

3Conclusion

Restate thesis (in different words) → Summarise main points from each paragraph

Essay topics: (1) CAUSES of Industrial Revolution in Britain OR (2) CONSEQUENCES of Industrial Revolution in Britain. You will get ONE of the two.

DEMO: OPVL Analysis — Liberty Leading the People ▼

OOrigin

Painted by Eugène Delacroix, a French Romantic painter, in 1830. The painting commemorates the July Revolution of 1830 in France.

PPurpose

To celebrate the revolutionary spirit and the July Revolution of 1830. It is a political and artistic statement glorifying the fight for freedom and the overthrow of repressive rule.

VValue

It is a contemporary source (painted 1830, close to the 1789 French Revolutionary tradition). It vividly depicts the symbols and emotions of the revolutionary period. Shows that the ideals of 1789 (liberty, nationalism) were still alive in 1830. Useful for understanding revolutionary ideology and public sentiment.

LLimitations

It is a politically motivated painting — not an objective historical record. Only presents one (positive) perspective of the revolution. Delacroix may have been influenced by his own political views and the desire to glorify the revolution. Does not show the suffering or negative consequences of revolution.
